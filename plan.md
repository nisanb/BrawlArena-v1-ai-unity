Core combat loop

Move → aim → commit → dodge → recover → re-engage

Every attack should create a meaningful decision:

Spend ammo now or save it
Push forward or hold cover
Use an ability to secure a kill or escape
Chase an enemy or return to the objective

The game should reward positioning and teamwork more than raw button speed.

Recommended control system

Use dual-stick controls:

Left thumb: movement
Right thumb: aim and attack
Tap attack button: auto-aim at the nearest reasonable target
Drag attack button: manual aim
Separate buttons for:
Main attack
Ability
Ultimate
Optional gadget or utility skill

Auto-aim should help beginners, but manual aim must be stronger in skilled play.

Best combat structure
1. Limited ammo with automatic reload

Give each brawler around 3 attack charges.

This creates natural combat rhythm:

Burst damage
Retreat behind cover
Reload
Re-enter combat

Avoid unlimited basic attacks. Constant spam makes fights noisy and removes tactical timing.

2. Short time-to-kill, but not instant

A healthy target should usually survive:

3–5 normal hits
One coordinated team burst
A strong ultimate plus follow-up

A typical fight should last long enough for dodging, counterplay, and teammates to react.

3. Strong projectile readability

Players must quickly understand:

Who attacked
Where the attack is going
How dangerous it is
Whether it can pass through walls
Whether it has splash damage

Use distinct shapes, speeds, sounds, and warning indicators.

4. Cover-based maps

The map should be part of combat, not decoration.

Include:

Solid walls
Bushes or vision blockers
Narrow choke points
Open risk zones
Side routes
Central objective areas

Players should constantly decide between safety, vision, and objective pressure.

Team composition

Build around three broad roles:

Frontliner: controls space and absorbs pressure
Damage dealer: applies reliable damage
Support/controller: healing, slowing, shielding, zoning, or crowd control

Do not force strict roles, but balanced teams should naturally perform better.

Each brawler should have:

One clear strength
One obvious weakness
One recognizable combat pattern
At least one way to help teammates
Ability design

A good brawler kit usually contains:

Basic attack

The attack used most often. It should define the character.

Examples:

Straight projectile
Shotgun spread
Piercing beam
Returning projectile
Area explosion
Melee combo
Tactical ability

A short-cooldown tool that changes positioning or creates opportunities.

Examples:

Dash
Shield
Heal
Trap
Pull
Knockback
Temporary invisibility
Deployable object
Ultimate

The ultimate should create a major moment, not simply deal more damage.

Good ultimates:

Start team fights
Break defensive positions
Save teammates
Capture or deny an objective
Temporarily reshape the battlefield

Charge the ultimate through active gameplay, especially dealing damage or supporting allies. Avoid charging it purely over time.

Best objective mode for the main game

The strongest primary mode is usually a central objective with continuous pressure.

A good example:

A control zone appears in the middle
Teams earn points while controlling it
Leaving the zone stops scoring
First team to reach the score limit wins
Overtime removes some cover or expands the zone

This creates frequent fights and prevents players from hiding after gaining a small lead.

Other good modes:

Collect-and-hold resources
Escort payload
Score a ball into the enemy goal
Two-stage objective attack and defense

Avoid making standard team deathmatch your main mode. Objectives create more teamwork, movement, and strategic variety.

Death and respawning

Use:

Fast respawns, approximately 5–8 seconds
Brief spawn protection
Clear respawn locations
No long walks back to combat
Short match duration, approximately 3–5 minutes

Deaths should matter, but one early death should not decide the entire match.

Mobile-friendly depth

The ideal mobile brawler is:

Easy to control
Hard to position correctly
Easy to understand
Hard to master

Put complexity into:

Aim prediction
Ammo management
Ability timing
Map awareness
Team coordination
Objective control

Do not put complexity into excessive buttons or complicated combos.

My recommended complete formula

For a first playable version, build:

3v3 matches
One central control-zone mode
3-minute matches
Dual-stick movement and aiming
Auto-aim on tap
Three regenerating ammo charges
One tactical ability
One chargeable ultimate
Destructible or interactive cover
Fast respawning
Six brawlers with clearly different roles
No equipment system during the first prototype

The most important design principle is:

Every brawler should be simple to operate, but every fight should create difficult decisions.

Start by making movement, aiming, hit feedback, and dodging feel excellent before adding progression, skins, rankings, or many characters.
